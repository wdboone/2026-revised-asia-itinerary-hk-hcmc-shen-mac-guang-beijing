üõÇVisa Requirements

  • Vietnam: e-Visa available (apply online 3 days before departure). Valid for 90 days single entry. Processing usually takes 3 business days.
  • China (Mainland): Tourist visa (L-Visa) required in advance OR check for 144-hour transit visa exemption if applicable. Verify current requirements at Chinese embassy/consulate.
  • Hong Kong: US citizens visa-free for 90 days. Valid passport required (at least 6 months validity).
  • Macau: US citizens visa-free for 30 days. Valid passport required.

üì±Internet & Connectivity

  • CRITICAL FOR CHINA: VPN required for accessing Google, WhatsApp, Facebook, Instagram, and Gmail. Install and test VPN BEFORE arriving in China!
  • Consider international phone plans from your carrier (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile offer Asia packages).
  • Alternative: Purchase local SIM cards at airports for data connectivity.
  • Download offline maps (Google Maps, Maps.me) before traveling.

üìÖImportant Booking Tips

  • Forbidden City (Beijing): MUST book tickets online in advance through official website. Tickets sell out daily, especially during peak season. Book at least 7 days ahead.
  • Great Wall of China: Highly recommended to book organized tour or private driver. Much easier than navigating public transportation. Mutianyu section is less crowded than Badaling.
  • Peak Tram (Hong Kong): Buy Sky Pass combo ticket online to skip extremely long queues (can be 2+ hours).
  • TurboJet Ferry (Hong Kong-Macau): Book tickets in advance online for better prices and guaranteed seats.

üå§Ô∏èWeather & Climate (Late May - Early June)

  • Vietnam & Hong Kong: Hot and humid (80-90¬∞F / 27-32¬∞C). High humidity levels. Afternoon rain showers possible.
  • Beijing: Warm and pleasant (70-85¬∞F / 21-29¬∞C). Less humid than southern regions. Occasional rain.
  • What to Expect: Monsoon season beginning in Southeast Asia. Bring rain gear and breathable clothing.

üì≤Essential Apps to Download

  • Before Departure (Critical):
    • VPN app (ExpressVPN, NordVPN, Surfshark) - essential for China
    • Google Maps (download offline maps for all cities)

‚öïÔ∏èHealth & Safety

  • Check CDC travel health notices and recommended vaccinations before departure.
  • Travel insurance with medical coverage is highly recommended.
  • Drink bottled water only in Vietnam and China (tap water not safe to drink).
  • Pack basic medications (pain relievers, anti-diarrheal, antihistamines).

üáªüá≥Vietnam - Vietnamese Dong (VND)

  • Symbol: ‚Ç´
  • Approximate Exchange Rate: 1 USD ‚âà 24,000-25,000 VND
  • Cash Tips: ATMs widely available in cities. Withdraw at bank ATMs for best rates. Many places accept USD but give change in VND.

  • Recommendations: Carry mix of VND and USD. Notify your bank before traveling to avoid card blocks.

  • Symbol: HK$ or $

  • Cards: Credit cards widely accepted. Contactless payment very common.
  • ATMs: Abundant throughout the city with English language options.
  • Recommendations: Get Octopus Card at airport upon arrival. Most transactions can be cashless.

üá≤üá¥Macau - Macanese Pataca (MOP)

  • Symbol: MOP$ or P
  • Approximate Exchange Rate: 1 USD ‚âà 8.0 MOP
  • Important: Hong Kong Dollars (HKD) are widely accepted everywhere in Macau at 1:1 ratio, so no need to exchange currency specifically for Macau.
  • Cards: Widely accepted at casinos, hotels, and major establishments.
  • Recommendations: Just use your Hong Kong Dollars for the day trip to Macau. Save time and exchange fees.

üá®üá≥China - Chinese Yuan Renminbi (CNY/RMB)

  • Symbol: ¬• or ÂÖÉ
  • Approximate Exchange Rate: 1 USD ‚âà 7.2 CNY
  • Digital Payments: Alipay and WeChat Pay dominate in China. Cash becoming less common but still necessary.
  • Foreign Cards: Setting up Alipay/WeChat Pay as a foreigner can be complex. Carry sufficient cash.
  • ATMs: Available but some may not accept foreign cards. Use Bank of China ATMs for best compatibility.
  • Recommendations: Withdraw cash at airport or major bank ATMs. Hotels and tourist sites accept cards. Small vendors and taxis prefer cash.

üí≥General Money Tips

  • Notify your bank/credit card companies of travel dates and destinations to avoid fraud blocks.
  • Use credit cards with no foreign transaction fees (many travel cards offer this).
  • ATMs generally offer better exchange rates than currency exchange counters.
  • Keep small bills/coins handy for taxis, tips, and small purchases.
  • Download currency converter app for quick reference.
  • Keep emergency cash (USD) separate from daily spending money.
üöá Transportation Guide
‚àí

üáªüá≥Ho Chi Minh City Transportation

  • Grab (Ride-Hailing): Most convenient and safe option. Like Uber - book through app, fixed prices, English interface. Available for cars and motorbikes.
  • Taxis: Use reputable companies (Vinasun, Mai Linh - green or white cars). Insist on meter usage. Have hotel address in Vietnamese.
  • Cyclos & Xe Om (Motorbike Taxis): Negotiate price before riding. Fun for short distances but agree on price first!
  • Walking: District 1 is very walkable. Be cautious crossing streets - traffic is chaotic but flows continuously. Walk confidently and steadily.
  • Bus: Very cheap but routes can be confusing for tourists. Google Maps helps but Grab is easier.
  • Tips: Download Grab app before arriving. Traffic is intense - allow extra time for transfers.

üá≠üá∞Hong Kong Transportation

  • MTR (Metro): World-class subway system. Fast, clean, efficient, air-conditioned. English signage everywhere. Get an Octopus Card!
  • Octopus Card: Rechargeable card for MTR, buses, trams, ferries, and even convenience stores. Purchase at airport or any MTR station.
  • Trams (Ding Ding): Historic double-decker trams on Hong Kong Island. Scenic, cheap (HK$3), and iconic. Payment by Octopus Card.
  • Star Ferry: Ferry between Kowloon and Hong Kong Island. Scenic, cheap (HK$3-4), and a must-do experience!
  • Buses: Extensive network. Double-decker buses offer great views. Use Octopus Card.
  • Taxis: Color-coded by region (Red: urban, Green: New Territories, Blue: Lantau). Relatively affordable. Most drivers speak limited English.
  • Peak Tram: Historic funicular railway to Victoria Peak. Book tickets online in advance to skip queues.
  • Airport Express: Fast train from airport to city (24 minutes). More expensive than alternatives but very convenient.

üö¢Hong Kong ‚Üî Macau (TurboJet Ferry)

  • Route: Hong Kong-Macau Ferry Terminal (Sheung Wan) to Macau Outer Harbour Terminal
  • Duration: Approximately 1 hour
  • Frequency: Ferries every 15-30 minutes throughout the day
  • Booking: Book online in advance for better rates and seat selection at www.turbojet.com.hk
  • Tips: Arrive 30 minutes before departure. Bring passport for immigration. Can get choppy in rough weather.

üöÑHong Kong ‚Üî Guangzhou (High-Speed Rail)

  • Departure: West Kowloon Station (Hong Kong)
  • Arrival: Guangzhou South Station
  • Duration: Approximately 47-50 minutes
  • Booking: Book tickets online or at station. Passport required for cross-border travel.
  • Tips: Very fast and comfortable. Clear immigration at Hong Kong station before boarding.

üöáHong Kong ‚Üî Shenzhen (MTR/Metro)

  • Route: Take MTR to Lo Wu or Lok Ma Chau stations (border crossings)
  • Border Crossing: Walk through immigration (bring passport). Can take 30-60 minutes depending on crowds.
  • Luohu Commercial City: Major shopping area immediately across border from Lo Wu station
  • Alternative: High-speed rail from West Kowloon to Shenzhen (faster but more expensive)
  • Tips: Weekdays are less crowded at border. Avoid peak hours (8-9am, 5-7pm).

üá®üá≥Beijing Transportation

  • Beijing Metro: Extensive subway system with 20+ lines. Very affordable. English signage. Get a rechargeable transit card or use mobile payments.
  • Didi (Ride-Hailing): Chinese equivalent of Uber. Download app and set up before arrival. English interface available.
  • Taxis: Abundant and affordable. Most drivers don't speak English - have destinations written in Chinese characters.
  • Buses: Cheap but routes complex for tourists. Metro is easier.
  • Walking: Many tourist sites are far apart. Metro + walking combination works best.
  • Tours: For Great Wall, organized tours or private drivers highly recommended.
  • Tips: Download Baidu Maps (works without VPN). Have hotel address in Chinese for taxi drivers.

Its architecture reflects layers of history‚Äîfrom ancient Vietnamese temples to grand French colonial buildings and modern skyscrapers. The city was renamed after Vietnamese revolutionary leader Ho Chi Minh following the reunification of Vietnam. Character: Dynamic, energetic, chaotic yet charming. The city buzzes with over 9 million people and countless motorbikes creating a unique urban symphony. Known for incredible street food, coffee culture, and entrepreneurial spirit.

üåÉ Hong Kong, China

History: Hong Kong's story is one of remarkable transformation. Originally a fishing village, it became a British colony in 1842 following the First Opium War. For 156 years under British rule, Hong Kong developed into a major international financial center, blending Eastern and Western influences in unique ways. This historical journey created a unique identity‚Äîa Chinese city with British influences, where traditional temples share streets with ultramodern skyscrapers, dim sum is served alongside afternoon tea, and Cantonese mingles with English.

Character: Fast-paced, vertical city where mountains meet harbor. Known for incredible skyline, world-class dining, efficient public transport, and unique blend of tradition and modernity. Shopping paradise and food lover's dream.

üé∞ Macau, China

History: Macau holds the distinction of being the oldest European settlement in Asia, established as a Portuguese trading post in 1557. For over 400 years, it remained under Portuguese administration, creating a unique cultural blend that earned it UNESCO World Heritage status. Returned to China in 1999 as a Special Administrative Region, Macau transformed into the "Las Vegas of Asia," with casino revenue surpassing Las Vegas. Yet beyond the glittering casinos, the old city preserves its Portuguese colonial charm through pastel buildings, cobblestone squares, and fusion cuisine (Macanese food blends Chinese, Portuguese, and Southeast Asian influences). This tiny peninsula became a bridge between East and West, and that legacy remains visible in every corner.

Character: Compact city of contrasts‚Äîhistoric Portuguese architecture meets mega-casino resorts. UNESCO heritage sites, fusion Macanese cuisine, and a unique blend of Chinese and Portuguese culture that exists nowhere else.

üèõÔ∏è Guangzhou, China

History: Guangzhou, known historically as Canton, is one of China's oldest and most important cities, with over 2,200 years of history. As the starting point of the ancient Maritime Silk Road, it has been China's gateway to international trade for centuries. During the Qing Dynasty, Guangzhou was the only Chinese port permitted to conduct foreign trade, making it the first point of contact between China and the Western world. This cosmopolitan legacy continues today‚ÄîGuangzhou is China's third-largest city, the capital of Guangdong Province, and a major commercial hub. The city is famous as the birthplace of Cantonese cuisine (including dim sum), the center of Cantonese culture and language, and home to the historic Canton Fair, the largest trade fair in China.

Character: Commercial powerhouse with deep history. Famous for dim sum and Cantonese food culture. Modern skyline with preserved colonial architecture on Shamian Island. More authentic Chinese experience than Hong Kong.

üè¢ Shenzhen, China

History: Shenzhen is China's miracle city, transformed from a small fishing village of 30,000 people into a megacity of over 12 million in just 40 years. In 1980, Deng Xiaoping designated Shenzhen as China's first Special Economic Zone, making it the testing ground for economic reforms and opening up to foreign investment. It's younger than many of its residents, with cutting-edge architecture, efficient infrastructure, and a forward-thinking atmosphere. Shenzhen proves that cities can be built, not just grown over centuries.

Character: China's youngest megacity and tech hub. Ultra-modern skyline, innovative architecture, massive shopping complexes. Known for electronics markets and as manufacturing capital. Very different from traditional Chinese cities.

üèØ Beijing, China

History: Beijing has served as China's capital for over 800 years, witnessing the rise and fall of dynasties and the birth of modern China. The city's name means "Northern Capital," and it has been the political and cultural heart of China since the Ming Dynasty (1368-1644). Beijing is home to six UNESCO World Heritage Sites, including the Forbidden City (the world's largest imperial palace complex), the Temple of Heaven, the Summer Palace, and sections of the Great Wall. The city hosted the 2008 Summer Olympics, which transformed its infrastructure and global image. Today, Beijing balances its role as China's political center with preservation of imperial history‚Äîwhere ancient hutongs (traditional alleyways) weave between modern skyscrapers, and revolutionary monuments stand near imperial temples.

Character: China's political and cultural capital. Home to iconic landmarks like Forbidden City and Great Wall. Traditional hutongs contrast with modern development. Rich in history, art, and Peking opera. Famous for Peking duck and imperial cuisine.

üôè Cultural Etiquette
+

üõï Temple & Religious Site Etiquette

  • Dress modestly - cover shoulders and knees (bring a scarf or shawl)
  • Remove shoes before entering temples (look for shoe racks or follow locals)
  • Speak quietly and turn phones to silent
  • Ask permission before taking photos of monks or religious ceremonies
  • Don't point feet toward Buddha statues (feet are considered unclean)
  • Don't touch religious artifacts or Buddha statues
  • Women should not touch monks or hand items directly to them

üçΩÔ∏è Dining Customs

  • Vietnam: Slurping noodles is acceptable and shows enjoyment
  • Wait for eldest person to start eating before beginning
  • Use chopsticks properly - don't stick them upright in rice (resembles funeral incense)
  • Try everything offered to you (it's polite even if just a small taste)
  • Tipping in Vietnam: 5-10% at restaurants, round up for taxis
  • Hong Kong: 10% service charge usually included, additional small tip appreciated
  • China: Tipping not customary except at high-end hotels/restaurants

  • Beckoning is done with palm down, fingers waving - palm up is rude
